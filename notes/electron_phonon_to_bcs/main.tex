\section{From the electron--phonon interaction to the BCS ground state}
\label{sec:electron-phonon-to-bcs}

\subsection{Motivation}

The chain of arguments leading from a lattice of ions and electrons to the BCS ground
state is long, and no single source spells out every step: the derivation of the
Fr\"ohlich Hamiltonian, the canonical transformation that eliminates the phonons, the
Cooper problem, the mean-field decoupling, the Bogoliubov diagonalization, and finally
the pairing wavefunction. This note gives the skeleton of the argument together with the
intermediate steps that are usually left as exercises: the choice of the generator $S$
and its matrix elements, the sign structure that makes the induced interaction
attractive, the evaluation of the Cooper and gap integrals, and the explicit verification
that the BCS state is the quasiparticle vacuum.

The presentation follows G.\ Czycholl, \emph{Theoretische Festk\"orperphysik} Band~2,
Ch.~5; equation numbers of the form (C\,5.x) refer to that book, so that the note can be
read alongside it. The starting Hamiltonian is derived in Band~1, Sec.~7.1, and in Bruus
\& Flensberg, Ch.~3.

Two of the steps below are similarity transformations in the sense of
Sec.~\ref{sec:similarity-transformations}, and are cross-referenced where they occur: the
phonon elimination is a case where the Hadamard series does \emph{not} close and must be
truncated, whereas the Bogoliubov transformation is a textbook case where it does.

\subsection{Physical picture}

An electron $e_1$ moving through the lattice attracts the positively charged ions and
leaves behind a region of enhanced positive charge density, i.e.\ a lattice distortion.
Because the ions are slow ($\omega_D \ll E_F/\hbar$), this distortion persists after
$e_1$ has moved on, and a second electron $e_2$ arriving later is attracted to it. The
net result is an effective electron--electron interaction, mediated by the exchange of a
phonon, that is attractive for electrons whose energies differ by less than a phonon
energy. The retardation is essential: it is what allows the attraction to beat the
(instantaneous, screened) Coulomb repulsion.

\subsection{Step 1: the Fr\"ohlich Hamiltonian}

The starting point is
\begin{align}
H &= \underbrace{\sum_{\vec k,\sigma}\varepsilon_{\vec k}\,
      c^\dagger_{\vec k\sigma}c_{\vec k\sigma}
    + \sum_{\vec q}\hbar\omega_{\vec q}\,b^\dagger_{\vec q}b_{\vec q}}_{\textstyle H_0}
  \nonumber\\
  &\quad + \underbrace{\sum_{\vec k,\vec q,\sigma}
      \Big[M(\vec q)\,c^\dagger_{\vec k+\vec q,\sigma}c_{\vec k\sigma}b_{\vec q}
      + M(-\vec q)\,c^\dagger_{\vec k-\vec q,\sigma}c_{\vec k\sigma}
        b^\dagger_{\vec q}\Big]}_{\textstyle H_1},
\label{eq:froehlich}
\end{align}
with $\omega_{-\vec q}=\omega_{\vec q}$ and $M(-\vec q)=M(\vec q)^*$ (the latter is forced
by $H=H^\dagger$ after relabelling $\vec k\to\vec k-\vec q$ in the second term; for the
jellium model $M$ can be chosen real, which is the convention used below). Each vertex
absorbs or emits one phonon, conserves spin along the fermion line, and transfers
momentum $\pm\vec q$.

\subsection{Step 2: eliminating the phonons (Schrieffer--Wolff)}

We want a purely electronic Hamiltonian, i.e.\ one that no longer contains processes
changing the phonon number. Apply a canonical transformation
\begin{equation}
H_T = e^{-iS}He^{iS},\qquad S^\dagger = S ,
\end{equation}
which is a similarity transformation with anti-Hermitian generator $A\equiv -iS$, so the
Hadamard lemma of Sec.~\ref{sec:similarity-transformations} applies:
\begin{equation}
H_T = H + [A,H] + \tfrac12\big[A,[A,H]\big] + \dots
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Choosing the generator.}
Split $H=H_0+H_1$ and demand that the transformation cancel $H_1$ at first order,
\begin{equation}
H_1 + [A,H_0] = 0 .
\label{eq:sw-condition}
\end{equation}
Everything at first order in the coupling is then gone, and collecting the remaining
terms,
\begin{equation}
H_T = H_0 + \tfrac12[A,H_1] + \mathcal O(H_1^3),
\label{eq:sw-result}
\end{equation}
where the factor $\tfrac12$ arises because $\tfrac12[A,[A,H_0]] = -\tfrac12[A,H_1]$
partially cancels $[A,H_1]$. Note the structure: \emph{because} $H_1$ has been removed at
first order, the leading effect is second order in $M$, which is exactly the
phonon-exchange process.

Equation \eqref{eq:sw-condition} is solved in the eigenbasis of $H_0$: taking matrix
elements of $[A,H_0]_{mn}=A_{mn}(E_n-E_m)$ gives
\begin{equation}
A_{mn} = \frac{(H_1)_{mn}}{E_m-E_n} .
\end{equation}
Every term of $H_1$ in \eqref{eq:froehlich} is an eigenoperator of $\ad_{H_0}$, so the
energy denominators can be read off directly:
\begin{equation}
A = \sum_{\vec k,\vec q,\sigma}\left[
\frac{M(\vec q)\,c^\dagger_{\vec k+\vec q,\sigma}c_{\vec k\sigma}b_{\vec q}}
     {\varepsilon_{\vec k+\vec q}-\varepsilon_{\vec k}-\hbar\omega_{\vec q}}
+\frac{M(\vec q)^*\,c^\dagger_{\vec k-\vec q,\sigma}c_{\vec k\sigma}b^\dagger_{\vec q}}
     {\varepsilon_{\vec k-\vec q}-\varepsilon_{\vec k}+\hbar\omega_{\vec q}}\right].
\end{equation}
The denominators are the energy cost of the corresponding virtual process. They are the
origin of the resonant denominator in the final interaction, and of the fact that the
transformation is ill-defined on resonance.

\paragraph{The effective interaction.}
Inserting $A$ into \eqref{eq:sw-result}, normal-ordering, and restricting to the
zero-phonon subspace (this is the step where one ``simply forbids'' states with different
phonon configurations from mixing) gives
\begin{equation}
H_{\mathrm{int}} = \sum_{\vec k,\vec k',\vec q}\sum_{\sigma\sigma'}
\frac{|M(\vec q)|^2\,\hbar\omega_{\vec q}}
     {(\varepsilon_{\vec k}-\varepsilon_{\vec k+\vec q})^2-(\hbar\omega_{\vec q})^2}\,
c^\dagger_{\vec k+\vec q,\sigma}c^\dagger_{\vec k'-\vec q,\sigma'}
c_{\vec k'\sigma'}c_{\vec k\sigma},
\label{eq:heff-explicit}
\end{equation}
which is Czycholl's (C\,5.25) written as
\begin{equation}
H_{\mathrm{int}} = -\frac12\sum_{\sigma\sigma'}\sum_{\vec k,\vec k',\vec q}
V_{\vec k,\vec k',\vec q}\;
c^\dagger_{\vec k+\vec q,\sigma}c^\dagger_{\vec k'-\vec q,\sigma'}
c_{\vec k'\sigma'}c_{\vec k\sigma},\qquad
V_{\vec k,\vec k',\vec q}=
\frac{2|M(\vec q)|^2\hbar\omega_{\vec q}}
{(\hbar\omega_{\vec q})^2-(\varepsilon_{\vec k}-\varepsilon_{\vec k+\vec q})^2}.
\end{equation}
The whole of superconductivity hangs on the sign of the denominator:
\begin{equation}
V_{\vec k,\vec k',\vec q}>0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
|\varepsilon_{\vec k}-\varepsilon_{\vec k+\vec q}|<\hbar\omega_{\vec q}\le\hbar\omega_D ,
\end{equation}
i.e.\ the interaction is attractive exactly for electrons within a Debye energy of one
another --- a thin shell around the Fermi surface --- and repulsive otherwise. The Debye
frequency enters as the maximum available phonon energy.

\paragraph{Reading the vertex.}
In the diagram for \eqref{eq:heff-explicit}, the wiggly line carries momentum $\vec q$ and
each fermion line conserves its spin. Hence the incoming line $(\vec k,\sigma)$ must
leave as $(\vec k+\vec q,\sigma)$ and $(\vec k',\sigma')$ as $(\vec k'-\vec q,\sigma')$.
Connecting $(\vec k,\sigma)$ to $(\vec k'-\vec q,\sigma')$ is \emph{not} a valid fermion
line for this term: it would require a momentum transfer
$\vec k'-\vec q-\vec k\neq\vec q$ in general, and a spin flip. That ``crossed''
assignment is the exchange process, which is a different term of the same sum (obtained
by relabelling $\vec q\to\vec k'-\vec q-\vec k$) and is therefore already included ---
drawing it as a second contraction of the same term would double count.

\paragraph{Caveat.}
This elimination is \emph{not} exact. $H_1$ is cubic in fermion/boson operators, so
$\ad_A$ does not close on a finite set of operators (cf.\ the scope discussion in
Sec.~\ref{sec:similarity-transformations}); the Hadamard series can only be truncated at
$\mathcal O(M^2)$. Discarding the frequency dependence of the exchanged phonon in
addition replaces a retarded interaction by an instantaneous one, which is what makes the
naive comparison with the Coulomb repulsion misleading.

\subsection{Step 3: the Cooper problem}

Does an arbitrarily weak attraction destabilize the Fermi sea? Cooper's model: take a
filled, inert Fermi sea
\begin{equation}
|\phi_0\rangle=\prod_{\varepsilon_{\vec k}\le\varepsilon_F,\,\sigma}
c^\dagger_{\vec k\sigma}|0\rangle
\end{equation}
and add two electrons just above it, interacting only with each other via a constant
attraction $V>0$ in the shell $E_F\le\varepsilon_{\vec k}\le E_F+\hbar\omega_D$:
\begin{equation}
H_{\mathrm{int}}=-\frac V2\sum_{\sigma\sigma'}\sum_{\vec k,\vec q}
c^\dagger_{\vec k+\vec q,\sigma}c^\dagger_{-\vec k-\vec q,\sigma'}
c_{-\vec k\sigma'}c_{\vec k\sigma}.
\end{equation}
Only pairs with opposite momenta and opposite spins are retained (zero total momentum
maximizes the phase space of scattering partners; the singlet is favoured because the
orbital part is then symmetric and the two electrons can sit on top of each other, where
the attraction acts). Make the variational ansatz
\begin{equation}
|\psi_{\sigma\sigma'}\rangle=\sum_{\vec k}a_{\sigma\sigma'}(\vec k)\,
c^\dagger_{\vec k\sigma}c^\dagger_{-\vec k\sigma'}|\phi_0\rangle ,
\end{equation}
and minimize $E=\langle\psi|H|\psi\rangle$ subject to
$\langle\psi|\psi\rangle\stackrel{!}{=}1$ --- this yields an \emph{upper bound} on the
energy of the bound state, which is all that is needed to prove instability.

\paragraph{Solving it.}
The stationarity condition is the two-body Schr\"odinger equation restricted to the shell,
\begin{equation}
2\varepsilon_{\vec k}\,a(\vec k)-V\sum_{\vec k'}a(\vec k')=E\,a(\vec k)
\;\Longrightarrow\;
a(\vec k)=\frac{VC}{2\varepsilon_{\vec k}-E},\qquad C\equiv\sum_{\vec k'}a(\vec k').
\end{equation}
Summing over $\vec k$ divides out $C$ and leaves the self-consistency condition
\begin{equation}
1=V\sum_{\vec k}\frac{1}{2\varepsilon_{\vec k}-E}
\;\simeq\;Vg_0\!\!\int_{E_F}^{E_F+\hbar\omega_D}\!\!\frac{d\varepsilon}{2\varepsilon-E}
=\frac{Vg_0}{2}\,
\ln\!\frac{2E_F+2\hbar\omega_D-E}{2E_F-E},
\end{equation}
where $g_0$ is the density of states \emph{per spin} at $E_F$, taken constant across the
thin shell. Writing $E=2E_F-\Delta_b$ and solving for the binding energy,
\begin{equation}
\Delta_b=\frac{2\hbar\omega_D}{e^{2/(Vg_0)}-1}
\;\xrightarrow[\ Vg_0\ll1\ ]{}\;2\hbar\omega_D\,e^{-2/(Vg_0)} .
\label{eq:cooper}
\end{equation}
So (C\,5.35): a bound state exists for \emph{arbitrarily small} $V>0$, lying below the
non-interacting two-electron energy $2E_F$. The result is non-analytic in $V$ --- no
order of perturbation theory in $V$ would ever produce it. Since the argument applies to
every pair near the Fermi edge, the normal Fermi sea is unstable, and one must treat the
pairing self-consistently for all electrons at once. That is BCS theory.

\subsection{Step 4: mean-field (BCS) Hamiltonian}

Simplify \eqref{eq:heff-explicit} by keeping only opposite momenta and opposite spins and
dropping the $\vec q$-dependence of $V$ (C\,5.38):
\begin{equation}
H=\sum_{\vec k\sigma}\varepsilon_{\vec k}c^\dagger_{\vec k\sigma}c_{\vec k\sigma}
-V\sum_{\vec k,\vec k'}
c^\dagger_{\vec k\uparrow}c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}
c_{-\vec k'\downarrow}c_{\vec k'\uparrow},
\qquad
|\varepsilon_{\vec k}-\varepsilon_{\vec k'}|\le\hbar\omega_D .
\end{equation}
Now decouple the quartic term in the pair channel, $AB\approx\langle A\rangle B+
A\langle B\rangle-\langle A\rangle\langle B\rangle$, with the pair amplitude
\begin{equation}
\Delta = V\sum_{\vec k}\langle c_{-\vec k\downarrow}c_{\vec k\uparrow}\rangle .
\label{eq:gap-def}
\end{equation}
Using $c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}c_{-\vec k\downarrow}
=\mathds{1}-c_{-\vec k\downarrow}c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}$ to bring the kinetic term
into Nambu form gives (C\,5.41)
\begin{equation}
H_{\mathrm{MF}}=\sum_{\vec k}
\begin{pmatrix}c^\dagger_{\vec k\uparrow}&c_{-\vec k\downarrow}\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}\varepsilon_{\vec k}&-\Delta\\-\Delta^*&-\varepsilon_{\vec k}\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}c_{\vec k\uparrow}\\c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}\end{pmatrix}
+\sum_{\vec k}\varepsilon_{\vec k}+\frac{|\Delta|^2}{V}.
\end{equation}
Here $\varepsilon_{\vec k}$ must be measured from the chemical potential,
$\varepsilon_{\vec k}\to\varepsilon_{\vec k}-\mu$, since $H_{\mathrm{MF}}$ does not
conserve particle number and one is really working in the grand canonical ensemble.

\subsection{Step 5: Bogoliubov diagonalization}

Introduce a unitary $U_{\vec k}$ diagonalizing the $2\times2$ matrix, so that
$H_{\mathrm{MF}}$ contains only number operators of new fermions. Taking $\Delta$ real
and positive (its phase can be absorbed into the fermion operators) the eigenvectors,
normalized to one, give (C\,5.43)
\begin{equation}
\alpha_{\vec k}=u_{\vec k}c_{\vec k\uparrow}-v_{\vec k}c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow},
\qquad
\beta_{\vec k}=u_{\vec k}c_{-\vec k\downarrow}+v_{\vec k}c^\dagger_{\vec k\uparrow},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
u_{\vec k}^2=\frac12\Big(1+\frac{\varepsilon_{\vec k}}{E_{\vec k}}\Big),\qquad
v_{\vec k}^2=\frac12\Big(1-\frac{\varepsilon_{\vec k}}{E_{\vec k}}\Big),\qquad
2u_{\vec k}v_{\vec k}=\frac{\Delta}{E_{\vec k}},\qquad u^2_{\vec k}+v^2_{\vec k}=1 .
\label{eq:uv}
\end{equation}
Because $U_{\vec k}$ is unitary, the new operators are again fermionic,
$\{\alpha_{\vec k},\alpha^\dagger_{\vec k'}\}=\delta_{\vec k\vec k'}$, and hence
$\langle\alpha^\dagger_{\vec k}\alpha_{\vec k}\rangle=f(E_{\vec k})$ (Fermi function)
while anomalous averages such as $\langle\beta_{\vec k}\alpha_{\vec k}\rangle$ vanish.
The result is (C\,5.46)--(C\,5.47)
\begin{equation}
H_{\mathrm{eff}}=\sum_{\vec k}E_{\vec k}
\big(\alpha^\dagger_{\vec k}\alpha_{\vec k}+\beta^\dagger_{\vec k}\beta_{\vec k}\big)
+\mathrm{const},
\qquad
E_{\vec k}=\big(\varepsilon_{\vec k}^2+|\Delta|^2\big)^{1/2}\ge0 ,
\end{equation}
with $\mathrm{const}=\sum_{\vec k}(\varepsilon_{\vec k}-E_{\vec k})+|\Delta|^2/V$. The
excitation spectrum is gapped: $E_{\vec k}\ge|\Delta|$ for all $\vec k$.

\paragraph{As a similarity transformation.}
The same result follows from Method~B of Sec.~\ref{sec:similarity-transformations}. Take
\begin{equation}
S=\sum_{\vec k}\theta_{\vec k}
\big(c^\dagger_{\vec k\uparrow}c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}
-c_{-\vec k\downarrow}c_{\vec k\uparrow}\big),\qquad S^\dagger=-S .
\end{equation}
Then $\ad_S$ closes on the pair $\{c_{\vec k\uparrow},c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}\}$,
\begin{equation}
[S,c_{\vec k\uparrow}]=-\theta_{\vec k}c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow},\qquad
[S,c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}]=+\theta_{\vec k}c_{\vec k\uparrow},
\end{equation}
so $M=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{smallmatrix}\right)$ is elliptic and the flow
is a rotation,
\begin{equation}
e^{S}c_{\vec k\uparrow}e^{-S}
=\cos\theta_{\vec k}\,c_{\vec k\uparrow}
-\sin\theta_{\vec k}\,c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow},
\end{equation}
i.e.\ $u_{\vec k}=\cos\theta_{\vec k}$, $v_{\vec k}=\sin\theta_{\vec k}$ with
$\tan2\theta_{\vec k}=\Delta/\varepsilon_{\vec k}$, reproducing \eqref{eq:uv}. This is the
fermionic counterpart of the (hyperbolic) bosonic squeezing transformation; the compact
rotation instead of a boost is the signature of the anticommuting algebra.

\subsection{Step 6: the gap equation}

Feed the diagonalization back into the definition \eqref{eq:gap-def}. Inverting,
$c_{\vec k\uparrow}=u_{\vec k}\alpha_{\vec k}+v_{\vec k}\beta^\dagger_{\vec k}$ and
$c_{-\vec k\downarrow}=u_{\vec k}\beta_{\vec k}-v_{\vec k}\alpha^\dagger_{\vec k}$, so
\begin{equation}
\langle c_{-\vec k\downarrow}c_{\vec k\uparrow}\rangle
=u_{\vec k}v_{\vec k}\big(1-2f(E_{\vec k})\big)
=\frac{\Delta}{2E_{\vec k}}\tanh\!\Big(\frac{\beta E_{\vec k}}{2}\Big),
\end{equation}
($\beta=1/k_BT$ here --- note the unfortunate clash with the Bogoliubov operator
$\beta_{\vec k}$), giving the BCS self-consistency equation (C\,5.50)
\begin{equation}
\Delta=\frac{V\Delta}{2}\sum_{\vec k}\frac{1}{E_{\vec k}}
\tanh\!\Big(\frac{\beta E_{\vec k}}{2}\Big).
\end{equation}
$\Delta=0$ always solves this. For $\Delta\neq0$ divide through (C\,5.51):
\begin{equation}
1=\frac V2\sum_{\vec k}^{|\varepsilon_{\vec k}|<\hbar\omega_D}
\big(\varepsilon_{\vec k}^2+|\Delta|^2\big)^{-1/2}
\tanh\!\left(\frac{\beta\big(\varepsilon_{\vec k}^2+|\Delta|^2\big)^{1/2}}{2}\right).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Two limits worth having explicitly.}
At $T=0$ the $\tanh\to1$ and, with $g_0$ constant,
\begin{equation}
1=\frac{Vg_0}{2}\int_{-\hbar\omega_D}^{\hbar\omega_D}
\frac{d\varepsilon}{\sqrt{\varepsilon^2+\Delta^2}}
=Vg_0\,\mathrm{arcsinh}\frac{\hbar\omega_D}{\Delta}
\;\Longrightarrow\;
\Delta(0)=\frac{\hbar\omega_D}{\sinh\big(1/(Vg_0)\big)}\simeq2\hbar\omega_D e^{-1/(Vg_0)} .
\end{equation}
Setting instead $\Delta\to0$ locates the critical temperature,
$k_BT_c\simeq1.13\,\hbar\omega_D e^{-1/(Vg_0)}$, whence the parameter-free BCS ratio
$2\Delta(0)/k_BT_c\simeq3.53$.

Comparing with the Cooper result \eqref{eq:cooper}: the exponents differ by a factor of
two, $e^{-1/(Vg_0)}$ versus $e^{-2/(Vg_0)}$. The Cooper binding energy of a single pair
on top of a rigid Fermi sea is therefore \emph{not} the gap; the self-consistent
treatment, in which all pairs form simultaneously and the sea rearranges, is
parametrically more strongly bound.

\paragraph{Why $\Delta$ is an order parameter.}
In a normal metal $\langle c_{\vec k\uparrow}c_{-\vec k\downarrow}\rangle=0$ for all
$\vec k$, because a state of sharp particle number gives zero expectation to an operator
that changes $N$ by two; hence $\Delta=0$. A solution with $\Delta\neq0$ therefore signals
a state that is not an eigenstate of $N$, i.e.\ one with a condensate of Cooper pairs and
a spontaneously chosen phase. $\Delta$ is complex, and its phase is the broken $U(1)$
symmetry.

\subsection{Step 7: the BCS ground state}

Since $E_{\vec k}\ge0$ and $\alpha^\dagger_{\vec k}\alpha_{\vec k}$,
$\beta^\dagger_{\vec k}\beta_{\vec k}$ have eigenvalues $0,1$, the ground state of
$H_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is the state containing no quasiparticles:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_{\vec k}|\mathrm{BCS}\rangle=\beta_{\vec k}|\mathrm{BCS}\rangle=0
\quad\forall\,\vec k .
\end{equation}
This is satisfied by
\begin{equation}
|\mathrm{BCS}\rangle=\prod_{\vec k}
\big(u_{\vec k}+v_{\vec k}c^\dagger_{\vec k\uparrow}c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}\big)
|0\rangle
=\bigotimes_{\vec k}
\big(u_{\vec k}+v_{\vec k}c^\dagger_{\vec k\uparrow}c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}\big)
|0\rangle_{\vec k}.
\label{eq:bcs}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Verification.}
Two facts make this a one-line check. First, each factor
$P_{\vec k}\equiv u_{\vec k}+v_{\vec k}c^\dagger_{\vec k\uparrow}
c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}$ is \emph{even} in fermion operators, hence commutes with
everything; so $\alpha_{\vec k}$ can be moved past all factors $P_{\vec k'}$,
$\vec k'\neq\vec k$, without sign bookkeeping and acts on $P_{\vec k}|0\rangle_{\vec k}$
alone. Second, within that single mode,
\begin{align}
u_{\vec k}c_{\vec k\uparrow}\,P_{\vec k}|0\rangle
&=u_{\vec k}v_{\vec k}\,c_{\vec k\uparrow}c^\dagger_{\vec k\uparrow}
   c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}|0\rangle
 =u_{\vec k}v_{\vec k}\,c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}|0\rangle,\\
-v_{\vec k}c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}\,P_{\vec k}|0\rangle
&=-u_{\vec k}v_{\vec k}\,c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}|0\rangle
\qquad\big(\text{the }v^2\text{ term dies on }(c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow})^2=0\big),
\end{align}
and the two cancel, so $\alpha_{\vec k}|\mathrm{BCS}\rangle=0$. The computation for
$\beta_{\vec k}$ is identical up to one anticommutation sign. The tensor-product form in
\eqref{eq:bcs} reflects that the Hilbert space factorizes into independent four-dimensional
blocks $\{|0\rangle,c^\dagger_{\vec k\uparrow}|0\rangle,
c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}|0\rangle,c^\dagger_{\vec k\uparrow}
c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}|0\rangle\}$, of which the pairing state occupies only the
empty and doubly occupied ones.

\paragraph{Properties.}
Normalization is automatic, $\langle\mathrm{BCS}|\mathrm{BCS}\rangle=
\prod_{\vec k}(u_{\vec k}^2+v_{\vec k}^2)=1$, and
\begin{equation}
\langle n_{\vec k\uparrow}\rangle=v_{\vec k}^2
=\frac12\Big(1-\frac{\varepsilon_{\vec k}}{E_{\vec k}}\Big),
\qquad
\langle c_{-\vec k\downarrow}c_{\vec k\uparrow}\rangle=u_{\vec k}v_{\vec k}
=\frac{\Delta}{2E_{\vec k}} .
\end{equation}
The step function of the Fermi sea is smeared over a width $\sim\Delta$ about
$\varepsilon_{\vec k}=0$, and $u_{\vec k}v_{\vec k}$ (the coherence factor) is peaked
exactly there: only the shell where occupation is uncertain contributes to pairing.
Particle number is not sharp, $\langle N\rangle=2\sum_{\vec k}v_{\vec k}^2$ with
$\langle\Delta N^2\rangle=4\sum_{\vec k}u_{\vec k}^2v_{\vec k}^2=\mathcal O(N)$, so
relative fluctuations are $\mathcal O(N^{-1/2})$ and harmless in the thermodynamic limit.

\paragraph{Remark: a pair-product wavefunction.}
Factoring out $\prod u_{\vec k}$ recasts \eqref{eq:bcs} as a coherent state of pairs,
\begin{equation}
|\mathrm{BCS}\rangle\propto\exp\!\Big(\sum_{\vec k}g_{\vec k}\,
c^\dagger_{\vec k\uparrow}c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}\Big)|0\rangle,
\qquad g_{\vec k}=\frac{v_{\vec k}}{u_{\vec k}},
\end{equation}
whose fixed-$N$ projection is the geminal (pair-product) wavefunction
$\big(\sum_{\vec k}g_{\vec k}c^\dagger_{\vec k\uparrow}
c^\dagger_{-\vec k\downarrow}\big)^{N/2}|0\rangle$. This is the form in which BCS pairing
is usually grafted onto variational many-body ans\"atze: a Pfaffian/geminal reference
state, optionally multiplied by a correlator or a neural-network amplitude.

\subsection{Pitfalls and conventions}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Sign of the Cooper result.} With $\Delta_b\equiv2E_F-E_{\mathrm{bs}}>0$ the
binding energy is positive, $\Delta_b\simeq+2\hbar\omega_D e^{-2/(Vg_0)}$; sources that
write $\Delta E\simeq-2\hbar\omega_D e^{-2/(Vg_0)}$ are defining
$\Delta E=E_{\mathrm{bs}}-2E_F$. Worth fixing one convention, as the two appear
interchangeably in the literature.
\item \textbf{Density of states.} $g_0$ above is per spin. Using the total DOS
($2g_0$) silently halves the exponents.
\item \textbf{$\varepsilon_{\vec k}$ vs.\ $\varepsilon_{\vec k}-\mu$.} From Step~4 onwards
energies are measured from the chemical potential; the gap equation is wrong otherwise.
\item \textbf{Overloaded $\beta$.} Inverse temperature and Bogoliubov quasiparticle share
the symbol in the standard notation.
\item \textbf{Validity.} The phonon elimination is second order in $M$ and breaks down on
resonance; the mean-field decoupling neglects pair fluctuations (fine in $d=3$ with the
long coherence length of conventional superconductors, not in general); and the Coulomb
repulsion, omitted throughout, must be screened and retardation-reduced (Morel--Anderson
$\mu^*$) before the net interaction is attractive.
\end{itemize}
